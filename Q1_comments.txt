Question 1.1 — Principal Component Analysis (PCA) and Log Returns

Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is a linear dimensionality-reduction technique that identifies orthogonal directions (principal components) in the feature space along which the observed data exhibit maximal variance. By projecting high-dimensional observations onto the leading principal components, PCA isolates dominant patterns of co-movement and compresses information into a smaller set of uncorrelated factors. In financial machine learning, PCA is widely used for factor analysis, risk decomposition, noise reduction, and feature extraction: it helps analysts and models distinguish systematic (common) sources of risk from idiosyncratic (stock-specific) noise, reduce the number of inputs to downstream models, and visualize the dominant modes of market behavior.

When applying PCA to financial time series, practitioners typically transform raw price series into logarithmic returns rather than using simple arithmetic returns. There are three principal reasons for this choice. First, log returns are time-additive: the log return over multiple periods equals the sum of single-period log returns. This additivity greatly simplifies aggregation and statistical modeling across horizons, and it is consistent with the linear algebraic assumptions underlying PCA, which treats observations as sums of components. Second, log returns are numerically symmetric with respect to gains and losses: a proportional loss and a proportional gain of the same magnitude have symmetric effects on log returns, whereas arithmetic returns produce asymmetry when compounded. This symmetry reduces skew and makes high/low movements behave more uniformly, which is desirable when extracting linear factors. Third, log returns are empirically closer to the normal distribution than raw prices or arithmetic returns—especially at daily frequency—making them more compatible with PCA’s reliance on covariance (or correlation) structure and Gaussian-based interpretations. Normality is not required for PCA to run, but approximate normality simplifies statistical inference (e.g., confidence statements about loadings) and often improves interpretability of the principal components as meaningful latent factors.

Question 1.2 — Data retrieval and Pearson correlation interpretation

To implement the empirical part of the exercise, one can use the yfinance API to obtain daily closing prices for the thirty constituent stocks of the Dow Jones Industrial Average for at least two years of trading. After forward- and backward-imputing occasional missing values and computing daily log returns r_t = ln(P_t / P_{t-1}), the Pearson correlation matrix of the return series quantifies pairwise linear co-movement between stocks. The correlation matrix is symmetric and bounded between −1 and +1; entries near +1 indicate that two stocks typically move together (in the same direction) on a day-to-day basis, while entries near −1 indicate opposite daily movements.

Interpretation of the correlation matrix is primarily qualitative and structural. A preponderance of moderately high positive correlations across many pairs suggests the presence of common market-wide influences—macroeconomic shocks, systematic investor sentiment, or liquidity-driven moves—that synchronously drive a large fraction of the cross-section. Sector clusters often appear as blocks of higher within-sector correlation (e.g., financials with financials, technology with technology), indicating that industry-specific fundamentals or news generate correlated returns beyond the market effect. Conversely, low or negative correlations between some sector pairs indicate potential diversification opportunities: combining assets from negatively correlated sectors can reduce portfolio variance. The overall distribution of pairwise correlations (mean, median, standard deviation) provides a compact summary of market integration: a higher average correlation implies greater commonality of returns and therefore less diversification benefit from holding multiple constituents of the same index.

Question 1.3 — Eigendecomposition, PC loadings, and economic interpretation

Eigendecomposition of the covariance (or correlation) matrix yields eigenvalues and eigenvectors: eigenvalues quantify the variance explained by each principal component (PC), while eigenvectors (commonly called loadings) express how original variables (stocks) contribute to each PC. Plotting the feature weights (loadings) for PC1 and PC2 using horizontal bar charts conveys two important diagnostics. If PC1 truly represents a market factor, its loadings will typically be of the same sign (most often positive) and of broadly similar magnitude across almost all stocks. That pattern indicates that PC1 captures a common mode in which nearly every stock moves together—a systematic exposure analogous to the market beta in CAPM—so daily shocks reflected by PC1 correspond to market-wide up or down moves. Conversely, if several stocks have near-zero or negative loadings while others are strongly positive, PC1 would be indicating a more complex structure and would be a weaker candidate for a pure market factor.

PC2 often represents the largest orthogonal contrast to PC1 and is frequently economically interpretable as a sectoral or style contrast (for example, Growth vs. Value, Cyclical vs. Defensive, or Technology vs. Consumer Staples). In practice, PC2 loadings will typically split stocks into positive and negative groups; the sign separation exposes which stocks (or sectors) tend to outperform when the PC2 factor is positive versus when it is negative. For instance, a positive PC2 loading for several high-growth technology firms and negative loadings for consumer staples suggests that PC2 captures a ‘‘risk-on / risk-off’’ or ‘‘growth vs. defensive’’ dimension. Interpreting PC2 therefore involves inspecting the largest positive and negative loadings, mapping those tickers to sectors or styles, and reasoning about the macro-financial drivers that could produce the observed contrast.

Question 1.4 — Variance decomposition, Scree plot, k for 95% retention, and implications

Variance decomposition uses the eigenvalues to compute the percentage of total variance explained by each principal component. A Scree plot displays these percentages (or eigenvalues) in descending order and helps identify the dominant components. The cumulative explained variance curve shows how many components are required to capture a desired fraction of the total variance. The dimensionality k needed to preserve 95% of the information is obtained by finding the smallest integer k such that the cumulative explained variance across the first k components is at least 95%.

The numeric value of k has an immediate economic interpretation in terms of market integration versus fragmentation. A small k (for example, k between 1 and 4) indicates that a few latent factors explain most of the variability across the thirty constituents: the market is highly integrated and systematic factors dominate; therefore, naive diversification across these index constituents yields diminishing marginal benefits because the dominant risks are common. By contrast, a larger k (for example, k greater than 8–10) implies that a substantial fraction of return variability is idiosyncratic or sector-specific, indicating fragmentation: many independent sources of variance remain after removing the leading common factors, which implies richer opportunities for risk reduction through cross-sectional diversification.

As a concrete illustrative note, suppose PC1 explains approximately 30% of the variance while PC2 through PC4 explain, say, another 25% cumulatively (these are illustrative numbers). Under that scenario, the first few components capture a substantial but not overwhelming fraction of variance; one might find k between 6 and 10 components are required to reach the 95% threshold. That outcome would suggest a mixed structure: a meaningful market factor exists (PC1) but there remains appreciable sectoral and idiosyncratic heterogeneity among the Dow constituents. In such a market, risk management should explicitly account for both the dominant common factors (e.g., using factor hedging) and the residual cross-sectional structure (e.g., sector tilts or stock-level selection) to achieve efficient diversification.

Implications for portfolio construction and risk management follow directly from this decomposition. If k is small, portfolio managers should prioritize strategies that manage exposure to the small number of pervasive systematic factors (for example, hedging market beta, controlling exposure to interest-rate or liquidity-driven factors). If k is large, managers can extract more diversification gains by constructing portfolios that mix across the multiple independent sources of variance, and more sophisticated stock-selection or sector-rotation strategies can meaningfully reduce portfolio volatility.
